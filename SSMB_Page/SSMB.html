GlobeTrotter

GlobeTrotter (also known as SSMB29) is shaping up to be one of the most ambitious films in Indian cinema, directed by the visionary S.S. Rajamouli and starring Mahesh Babu alongside Priyanka Chopra. The film is being crafted as a global-scale adventure that blends mythological depth with modern spectacle. Set to launch its grand teaser event at Ramoji Film City in front of nearly 50,000 fans, GlobeTrotter is being marketed not just as a movie, but as a worldwide cinematic experience. The first look of Priyanka as Mandakini—armed and powerful atop a cliff—has already set social media on fire.Rumors and fan theories are wild: some believe Mahesh Babu plays a fearless explorer with traits inspired by Lord Hanuman, traversing jungles across continents in search of a mystical artefact. Others say Priyanka’s Mandakini could be a rival agent or protector of that relic, while the villain—rumored to be a physically disabled genius—represents the duality of technology and mythology. The movie reportedly spans exotic locations like Africa, the Amazon, and India, tying ancient legends to a modern-day adventure narrative.

Rajamouli, known for RRR and Baahubali, is expected to redefine cinematic scale again, blending Indian mythological roots with global adventure aesthetics. From massive teaser screens to live fan events streamed worldwide, GlobeTrotter is not just a film—it’s an experience, a myth-meets-modernity odyssey where visuals, music, and storytelling merge into something larger than life. Fans across the world are calling it “India’s answer to Indiana Jones, but with the soul of Hanuman.”

The story canvas, while still under wraps, hints at layers of mythology, adventure, and world travel. Early logic suggests Mahesh Babu’s character may be an explorer or an artefact-hunter—someone who dives into jungles and ancient ruins, seeking a relic of immense power. This is fused with Indian mythic symbolism—rudraksh malas, trishuls, references to Hanuman-like traits—highlighting Rajamouli’s penchant for blending ancient spirituality and mass spectacle. Priyanka Chopra’s character, Mandakini, unveiled in a striking first look where she wears a mustard-yellow saree and wields a gun, standing on a cliff edge against wind-lashed terrain—an image that redefines “heroine in action” in Indian cinema. Then there’s the villain. The first reveal for Prithviraj Sukumaran shows him as Kumbha, seated in a high-tech wheelchair, dressed in all-black—“sinister” and “powerful”, according to reports.

This suggests a layered antagonist whose physical limitation (wheelchair) is juxtaposed with mental or technological superiority—a fresh twist in the typical hero-vs-villain dynamic. Visually and technically, GlobeTrotter seems designed as a global spectacle. From the South American jungles to the heart of Africa (as fan theories suggest),

the title itself hints at a narrative that crosses continents. And with Rajamouli’s well-known ability to stage grand set-pieces, massive VFX, and emotionally charged drama, audience expectations are soaring. The marketing push—including live reveals, global streaming tie-ups, and a teaser event of epic scale—reinforces that this isn’t just regional; it’s international in ambition. PINKVILLA

Let’s dive deeper into the probable story structure (speculative but grounded in clues): The hero (Mahesh) begins perhaps in India, discovering a clue or artefact tied to a myth—maybe an ancient scroll, maybe a symbol of Hanuman’s legacy, or some lost temple relic. This triggers a chain of events that sends him abroad—jungles of Africa, rainforests of South America, ancient ruins hidden in plain sight. Along the way he meets Mandakini (Priyanka), who initially may seem like an ally but could be more—a guardian of knowledge, or someone with her own agenda. Then we have Kumbha (Prithviraj) who stands in opposition: technology + myth combine in him, perhaps he seeks the same relic for darker ends. The conflict escalates, the locations shift, we cross rivers, climb temples, dodge traps, and finally reach a climax where mythology’s symbolism and modern stakes collide—earth-shattering spectacle meets personal sacrifice. Why this matters now: Indian cinema is evolving.

The appetite for large-scale experiences is growing. Audiences want more than songs and dance—they want immersive universes, characters they root for, s that feel epic. Rajamouli has consistently tapped that. With GlobeTrotter, the gamble is higher—and the payoff potentially bigger. Priyanka’s involvement adds star power and pan-India/Global appeal. Mahesh Babu brings his own fandom and technical fluency. The global scope, mythic resonance, live fan-events—they all combine for a major milestone.
